It is now time to start bringing together all of your work on your project into a final report. In this topic you will submit a draft for feedback from your tutors. You should incorporate, as appropriate, the feedback on the different parts that you have already obtained, through your peer reviews and your previous summative submissions, as well as any tutor advice that you have been given in discussion forums or webinars.

The project report should consist of six chapters, most of which will be based on writing you have been doing throughout the project process:

Introduction: this will explain the project concept and motivation for the project (this can be based on your proposal). This must also state which project template you are using.  

Literature review: this is a revised version of the document that you submitted for your second peer review.

Design: this is a revised version of the document that you submitted for your third peer review.

Implementation: this should describe the implementation of the project. This should follow the style of the Topic 6 peer review (but greatly expanded to cover the entire implementation), describing the major algorithms/techniques used, including an explanation of the most important parts of the code and a visual representation of the results (e.g. screenshots or graphs). 

Evaluation: describe the evaluation carried out (e.g. user studies or testing on data) and give the results. This should give a critical evaluation of the project as a whole making clear successes, failures, limitations and possible extensions.

Conclusion: This can be a short summary of the project as a whole but it can also bring out any broader themes you would like to discuss or suggest further work.

Writing project reports
Although much of what you are doing for the report takes forward material you have already created, in different submissions and activities throughout the module, it is important to take an overall view of the project report itself.

The purpose of the project report is to report your work. It is the one place where you demonstrate what you have done, and it needs to be effective. You cannot assume that the person reading your report, whether a potential employer, or an examiner, or anyone else, has seen all of your work this far, or even has seen your earlier submissions.  

One of the best ways to view your task is to put yourself in the position of your reader, and think about what they need to have included in the report in order to understand what you have done, and to appreciate the quality of the work you have achieved.  

You can assume that your reader knows the area, and that they are a computer scientist with reasonable knowledge across the domain; they are not a random member of the public with no knowledge.  There is a shared background in Computer Science.  But you can’t assume that they know any of what you have done, nor what is unique about your work.  You have to show them this.

A good way to think about this is to imagine that you are a curator.  Your project is what is being shown, and you have to think about what you include in the exhibition.  You don’t want to overload your reader with information that they don’t need.  Also, this wastes precious word count! 

Another important thing to take on board is that this is a report of what you have done.  The examiners don’t need to see your project plan.  That is behind you.  It may be relevant to mention, if, for example you are reflecting on ways in which things deviated from what you expected.  But examiners generally don’t need you to --- at this point --- talk about things that are in the past.  In line with this is the fact that the tense and approach of the writing is important. A final report should be written mostly in a way that makes it clear that the work has been completed.  Talking about things you plan to do makes the examiner think that you have not done them.  So be careful to convey in the way you need to.  Of course, in the concluding sections of the report, you may be proposing some suggestions for further work.  These would naturally be described as work you have not yet done. 

Finally, this is an academic report.  You need to do appropriate referencing and citation.  By now, you should know how to do this, and it is important that you put this into practice.  Another significant aspect of a high quality academic report is that it contains appropriate justification, with evidence.  You will describe decisions that you made; as part of that description, you need to explain why you made the particular choice, and that choice should be justified with evidence.  The same applies to interpretation of results, analysis, evaluation, etc.  All of the statements you make, and all of the claims you propose, must have appropriate justification.

You will have a chance to do some of this for your draft report, and obtain feedback from your tutor on this.  While it is not mandatory, and it does not count towards your overall grade, it is a really good idea to take advantage of this opportunity, and get feedback on how well you are doing all of the above, as well as suggestions of how to improve.  So, prepare the draft report over the next week or so, and have something ready to submit at the appropriate submission point.
